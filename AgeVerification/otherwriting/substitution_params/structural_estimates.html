Structural Estimates: Nested Logit Dissimilarity Parameter

AgeVerification project — March 2026 — Self-contained derivation; nested logit framework

1. The Point of the Model

Why we want a unitless substitution parameter

The heterogeneity event studies already show how minutes changed for heavy vs. light users, by age, and so on. But minutes are not comparable across groups. A gain of 0.5 min/wk for a heavy user with a pre-ban baseline of 30 min/wk is a tiny share response. The same 0.5 min/wk for a casual user with a baseline of 2 min/wk is enormous. We want a unitless parameter that measures the same economic object — the degree of within-adult preference clustering relative to outside-option substitution — across groups. That parameter is $\lambda \in (0,1]$, the nested logit dissimilarity parameter, and it can be estimated separately for every user group from their event-study betas and group-specific pre-ban shares.

What $\lambda$ means economically

$\lambda$ is the primitive of the nested logit model. It governs within-nest preference correlation:

  • $\lambda = 1$ (standard logit, IIA): Adult sites substitute for each other no more than for non-adult time. Every age-verification ban sends demand straight to all-other (AO) — no reshuffling within the adult nest.
  • $\lambda \to 0$ (perfect within-nest correlation): Adult sites are nearly perfect substitutes. Bans merely reshuffle demand across adult sites with essentially no reduction in total adult consumption.
  • $\lambda = 0.217$ (our estimate): Strong within-nest preference correlation. Adult sites are substantially closer substitutes for each other than for the outside option, but imperfect. The derived ratio $(1-\lambda)/\lambda = 3.61$.

Compared to a nested CES framing, $\lambda$ is cleaner: it is a single directly-identified primitive of the model. Nested CES required two elasticities ($\sigma_1$, $\sigma_2$) and only their combination $(\sigma_2-\sigma_1)/(\sigma_1-1)$ was identified; $\lambda$ alone is identified from the diversion ratio structure.

Preview of the heterogeneity payoff

The same formula applies by user group $g$. All inputs are from existing heterogeneity event studies plus group-specific pre-ban shares. The directional hypothesis is that heavier pre-ban adult users will have lower $\lambda^{(g)}$ — tighter within-adult preference clustering — while casual users have $\lambda^{(g)}$ closer to 1 and exit to all-other entirely.

2. Setup

The model has 7 goods inside a two-level nested logit (McFadden, 1978):

  • Adult nest $\mathcal{A}$: PornHub (PH), xHamster (XH), OXXX (an "Other XXX" bundle), Chaturbate (CB), xVideos (XV), xnxx (XNXX)
  • All-other (AO): the complement, constructed residually from a fixed time budget $T = 10{,}080$ min/wk

The single primitive is $\lambda \in (0,1]$, the dissimilarity parameter. Consumers draw utility $U_i = V_i + \varepsilon_i$ where the $\varepsilon_i$ follow a generalized extreme value distribution with correlation $1-\lambda$ within the adult nest. Lower $\lambda$ means stronger within-nest preference clustering.

The three sites directly affected by age-verification mandates are PH, XH, and OXXX (partially). Three simultaneous shocks of unknown magnitude hit in the same event window, so we cannot separately identify $\delta_\text{PH}$, $\delta_\text{XH}$, $\delta_\text{OXXX}$ from the aggregate event studies. This is not a problem, as shown in Section 4.

For each site $i$, $\beta_i$ denotes the long-term ATT (weighted average over $\tau = 4$–8), estimated on the win_min outcome. Pre-ban shares are $s_i = m_i / T$ where $m_i$ is the pre-ban mean in min/wk. $s_A = \sum_{i \in \text{adult}} s_i$ is the adult nest share of total time.

4. The Identification Argument

Three simultaneous shocks

Each observed event-study estimate picks up contributions from all three simultaneous shocks:

$$\beta_i \approx \gamma_{i,\text{PH}}\cdot\delta_\text{PH} + \gamma_{i,\text{XH}}\cdot\delta_\text{XH} + \gamma_{i,\text{OXXX}}\cdot\delta_\text{OXXX}$$

The shock magnitudes $\delta_j$ are unknown. This would seem to prevent structural estimation — but it does not, because of the nested logit factorization.

Nested logit Slutsky factorization

The nested logit Slutsky matrix has a key factorization property. For any adult site $i$ and any shocked adult site $j$ in the same nest ($i \neq j$, both $\in \mathcal{A}$):

$$\gamma_{ij} = \frac{s_i \cdot s_j}{s_A} \cdot \varphi_\lambda, \qquad \text{where } \varphi_\lambda \equiv \frac{1}{\lambda} - s_0 \approx \frac{1-\lambda}{\lambda} \text{ (since } s_0 \approx 1)$$

This factors as $f(i) \cdot g(j)$: a function only of $i$ times a function only of $j$. To see why this matters: for any unshocked adult site $i$ (XV or XNXX), the combined beta is:

$$\beta_i = \frac{s_i}{s_A} \cdot \varphi_\lambda \cdot \underbrace{\sum_{j \in \text{shocked}} s_j \cdot \delta_j}_{\equiv\, \Delta}$$

The factor $\Delta$ is the same for every unshocked site $i$ — it does not depend on $i$. For the outside option, the Slutsky term is $\gamma_{i,\text{AO}} = s_i s_0 \approx s_i$, so:

$$\beta_\text{AO} = \Delta \cdot s_0 \approx \Delta$$

(Here we use the adding-up identity $\beta_\text{AO} = -\beta_\text{AllXXX}$ and $s_0 \approx 1$.)

The key cancellation

$\Delta$ cancels in the ratio $\beta_i / \beta_\text{AO}$:

Key identification formula
$$\frac{\beta_i}{\beta_\text{AO}} = \frac{s_i}{s_A} \cdot \frac{1-\lambda}{\lambda}$$

Solving for $\lambda$: $$\lambda = \frac{1}{1 + \dfrac{\beta_i}{\beta_\text{AO}} \cdot \dfrac{s_A}{s_i}}$$

This holds for every unshocked adult site $i$, regardless of which sites are shocked, how many are shocked simultaneously, or the shock magnitudes $\delta_j$. Simultaneous shocks are fine because $\Delta$ cancels. $\lambda$ is a single directly-identified primitive — no normalization assumption is needed.

The identifying variation is the relative diversion of shocked-site traffic between unshocked adult sites (XV, XNXX) and all-other (AO). Whatever the total diversion $\Delta$, the fraction going to any adult site vs. AO depends only on shares and $\lambda$.

Proportionality test

Nested logit predicts that for any pair of unshocked adult sites $i, i'$, their relative gains should equal their relative pre-ban shares:

$$\frac{\beta_\text{XV}}{\beta_\text{XNXX}} = \frac{s_\text{XV}}{s_\text{XNXX}}$$
Proportionality test:
Predicted ratio: $s_\text{XV}/s_\text{XNXX} = 0.596/0.380 = 1.57$
Observed ratio: $\beta_\text{XV}/\beta_\text{XNXX} = 0.2451/0.0786 = 3.12$
Ratio-of-ratios: $3.12 / 1.57 = $ 1.99 → Reject proportionality

XV absorbed approximately twice as much diverted traffic as its share would predict relative to XNXX.

Why the answer is $\lambda = 0.217$, not 0.60

Common misreading to avoid
The raw ratio $(\beta_\text{XV} + \beta_\text{XNXX})/\beta_\text{AO} = 0.60$ is not the structural parameter $(1-\lambda)/\lambda$. It is the share of the total observed diversion that went to XV and XNXX specifically. Since XV and XNXX together account for only 16.7% of pre-ban adult time ($s_\text{XV} + s_\text{XNXX} = 0.976$ out of $s_A = 5.869$), their combined gain represents a small fraction of total within-adult reallocation. The share adjustment $s_A/(s_\text{XV} + s_\text{XNXX}) = 6.0$ scales up to what total within-adult substitution would be if all adult sites participated proportionally to their shares. The raw ratio is $(1-\lambda)/\lambda \cdot (s_\text{XV}+s_\text{XNXX})/s_A$, not $(1-\lambda)/\lambda$ itself.

6. Why $\lambda = 0.217$ Could Be Wrong

What would make the estimate too low (overestimates within-adult substitution, $\lambda$ too small)

1. OXXX contamination. If OXXX is partially shocked — mandates affect some sites bundled inside OXXX — then $\delta_\text{OXXX} \neq 0$ biases $\beta_\text{XV}$ and $\beta_\text{XNXX}$ upward relative to what pure within-adult substitution from PH and XH alone would produce. Under nested logit this bias is proportional: it scales $s_i$ uniformly for all unshocked sites. The proportionality test is unaffected, but the level of $(1-\lambda)/\lambda$ is inflated, pushing $\lambda$ below its true value.

2. CB reallocation. Chaturbate has a declining beta (−0.054). If the CB decline reflects complementarity rather than own-shock, users displaced from PH could not substitute to CB — they went to XV and XNXX instead, artificially boosting their betas and inflating $(1-\lambda)/\lambda$.

What would make the estimate too high ($\lambda$ too large, underestimates within-adult substitution)

1. Measurement attenuation. The win_min outcome winsorizes large minute values. Heavy users who switched completely from PH to XV — the most intensive within-adult substitution — may be truncated at the top, attenuating $\beta_\text{XV}$. This would push $(1-\lambda)/\lambda$ downward and $\lambda$ upward.

2. Extensive-margin exit. If some users quit adult content entirely rather than substituting, $\beta_\text{AO}$ is large while $\beta_\text{XV}$ and $\beta_\text{XNXX}$ are small. The ratio $(1-\lambda)/\lambda$ is low, but this reflects extensive-margin exit, not low within-adult substitutability conditional on staying. The structural $\lambda$ for the intensive margin is lower (stronger substitution) than 0.217.

The proportionality failure as a structural warning

The 1.99 ratio-of-ratios (XV vs. XNXX) suggests these two unshocked sites are not symmetric substitutes for the shocked sites. Under a more flexible demand system, we would need site-specific cross-effects. Geographic variation — states where only PH was mandated vs. states where both PH and XH were mandated — could separately identify these. Without that variation, the pooled $\lambda = 0.217$ is a share-weighted average. It is a well-defined estimand, but not guaranteed to equal either the XV-only ($\lambda = 0.183$) or XNXX-only ($\lambda = 0.308$) estimate.

7. Heterogeneity Payoff

The same identification argument applies by user group $g$. Define $\beta_i^{(g)}$ as the group-$g$ long-term ATT for site $i$, and $s_i^{(g)}$ as the group-$g$ pre-ban share of $T$. Then the group-specific dissimilarity parameter is:

$$\lambda^{(g)} = \frac{1}{1 + \dfrac{\beta_i^{(g)}}{\beta_\text{AO}^{(g)}} \cdot \dfrac{s_A^{(g)}}{s_i^{(g)}}}$$

All inputs come from existing heterogeneity event studies (betas by group) plus group-specific pre-ban means (shares by group). No new regressions are needed. This puts heavy vs. light users, age groups, income groups, etc. on a common unitless scale.

Directional hypothesis: Users with higher pre-ban adult consumption (heavy users, likely habitual consumers) have more-developed preferences over adult sites and will find substitutes within the nest. We therefore expect lower $\lambda^{(g)}$ (stronger within-adult clustering) for heavy pre-ban users, and $\lambda^{(g)}$ closer to 1 for light users who exit to AO when the ban hits.

This is the key empirical deliverable: converting heterogeneous minute-change estimates into a single economic parameter that is directly comparable across groups with very different baseline levels of consumption.
